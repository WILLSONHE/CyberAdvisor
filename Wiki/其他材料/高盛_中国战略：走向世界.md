### The Chinese exports story has evolved

The Chinese exports story has evolved 
 
 
Since its accession to the World Trade Organization (WTO) in 2001, China has been 
labeled as the World’s factory. Indeed, China’s share in global manufacturing 
value-added has surged from 11% in 2001 to 33% in 2024 (1.7pp annual increase), 
significantly contributing to the 11% GDP CAGR during the same period, and leading 
China’s ascent to the 2nd largest economy (from the 6th in 2001). That said, we believe 
that the “Made in China” narrative has evolved along three key dimensions that will 
carry consequential implications to the stock market: 
From DM to EM: Partly driven by the elevated US-China trade tensions and policy 1.
uncertainty, Chinese exporters have diversified their presence in European countries 
and Emerging Market economies. Exports to non-US countries have been growing at 
an estimated 7.5% CAGR since 2018, while exports to the US have declined by 0.6% 
annually during the same period. China’s trade with Belt and Road countries now 
account for 47% of the total trades, up from 32% in 2005. 
Moving up the value-added curve: The last decade has seen a notable shift in 2.
China’s goods exports towards advanced technological products. China’s share in 
global exports of traditional items like toys, textiles and furniture have decreased by 
10% over the past 15 years. Exports of machinery and electronics were the key 
growth drivers during 2010 to 2020, while exports of Electrical Equipment and the 
“New Three” — electric vehicles, lithium-ion batteries, and solar cells — have  
experienced rapid growth in recent years. 
From tradable goods to ODI and services/IP: Beyond traditional goods exports, 3.
China has strategically increased its overseas direct investment (ODI) over recent 
years, particularly within Belt & Road Initiative countries. This strategy enables 
Chinese companies to diversify supply chains, build production capacity closer to 
end markets, and enhance business resilience. Concurrently, China is increasingly 
exporting services, including online e-commerce and entertainment services, travel 
and consumer services, biotech in contract research and development.

### The case for Going Global

The case for Going Global 
 
 
The macro setup and thematic drivers for Chinese companies expanding overseas 
continue to look promising, underpinned by the following factors: 
A highly competitive Renminbi on an effective trade-weighted basis should remain 1.
a tailwind for exporters over coming quarters. Chinese policymakers, having learned 
the lessons from Japan’s lost decades, may prefer to keep the currency competitive 
to support exports. Our economists’ study indicates the Renminbi is still undervalued 
within our GSDEER and GSFEER currency frameworks, offering Chinese exporters a 
competitive advantage for global expansion. 
China’s leading position in global supply chains, particularly in raw materials and 2.
advanced manufacturing capabilities, makes it an indispensable player in any global 
supply chain reorganization. China’s cost advantages also enable Chinese companies 
to offer major manufactured products at a 15% to 60% discount compared to their 
global competitors. Furthermore, China dominates production capacity in emerging 
manufacturing sectors like solar, batteries, construction machinery, and electric 
vehicles (EVs), although some countries are constraining China’s exports to protect 
the domestic production. Efforts by western parties to change this will however take 
time. 
The imperative for Chinese companies to diversify away from a highly competitive 3.
local market is another significant driver. The nexus of overcapacity, intense 
competition, and disinflation has weighed on Chinese corporate earnings in recent 
years. Constrained by weak domestic demand, seeking new growth opportunities in 
less saturated international markets becomes a strategic priority for certain Chinese 
enterprises to maintain their overall growth and profitability profile. 
Cultural proximity also plays a role; over 50 million ethnic Chinese residing outside 4.
Chinese mainland, mainly in Asia and America, could facilitate the global expansion 
by offering a readily available talent pool with local knowledge and cultural insights. 
At the same time, they could serve as the early adopters and a crucial initial market 
for Chinese companies venturing abroad. 
Fundamentally and most importantly, Chinese products have evolved significantly, 5.
demonstrating both cost-effectiveness and quality competitiveness globally. 
China has become a competitive producer of technologically complex goods, 
including telecommunication equipment, solar panels, drones, etc, thanks to their 
commitment to R&D investments. Across a variety of sectors, Chinese products 
present a compelling value proposition. This competitiveness is evident in the rapid 
global adoption of Chinese brands and products. In 2024, 130 Chinese companies 
are listed in the Fortune Global 500 (v.s. 100 Chinese companies 10 years ago), with 
the automotive, high-tech, and internet companies showing strong growing 
momentum.

### Earnings boost (and tariff risks) from overseas expansion

Earnings boost (and tariff risks) from overseas expansion 
 
 
Overseas revenue representation of all Chinese listed companies and MSCI China 
constituents has increased from 14% and 11% in 2018 to 16% and 15% currently, 
primarily driven by the Auto, Retailing, and Capital Goods sectors. Furthermore, the 
revenue share of Chinese companies relative to their global competitors has increased at 
an annual rate of 0.4pp over the past 10 years. Looking ahead, we estimate the foreign 
revenue growth using three distinct approaches: 
Extrapolation of historical overseas revenue exposure growth: Overseas revenue 1.
of all Chinese listed companies grew fairly steadily from 13.6% in 2021 to 16% now, 
representing a 0.8pp annual increase. Should this growth trajectory persist, we 
project the overseas revenue of Chinese companies (excluding Financials) could 
reach 19.2% (20.7%) by 2028. This level would still be below that observed in other 
DM (53%) and EM (48%) countries, implying further upside in the long term. 
Prediction based on sensitivity to export growth: Empirical analysis indicates that 2.
the overseas revenue growth of Chinese listed companies has been highly correlated 
with, and has outpaced, the country’s export growth. Based on this historical 
sensitivity and our economists’ export growth forecasts (4% CAGR in nominal terms 
for the next three years), we forecast China non-Financial companies to grow their 
overseas revenue by around 13% on average annually in the next three years, 
potentially raising foreign revenue representation to 20.6% by 2028.  
Cross-sectional regression analysis of sector foreign revenue exposure and 3.
company fundamentals: We analyze the foreign revenue exposure of all MSCI AC 
World stocks, categorized by GICS industry group since 2014, to identify factors 
influencing overseas expansion. The cross-sectional regressions indicate that R&D 
intensity, asset turnover, and gross margin are significant determinants. R&D 
intensity is a more important driver for Chinese companies’s overseas expansion. If 
the companies have strong innovation capability, they are better positioned to 
accelerate revenue overseas growth and gain market share globally. Leveraging 
increasing R&D investment and a pricing advantage stemming from compressed 
margins, the model projects that MSCI China companies could continue growing 
overseas revenue by about 0.7pp to 2027. 
Averaging these estimates, we expect Chinese companies will continue to increase their 
overseas revenue exposure by 0.6pp annually. On average, Chinese exporters enjoy 
approximately 20% higher gross margins in overseas markets in 2024 due to competitive 
domestic pricing environment. We expect the margin differences to narrow gradually as 
the anti-involution policies could lead to domestic price recovery. Combining this 
projected overseas revenue growth with moderated margin premium, we estimate that 
global expansion could boost earnings of MSCI China companies by about 1.5% 
annually over the next three years.  
Tariff risks could slow Chinese companies’ overseas expansion and lower the 
profitability. However, comparing the overseas revenue and earnings sensitivities to 
tariff hikes during 2018/19 and year-to-date, we find that the Chinese companies have 
become more resilient with diversified supply chains and expanded end markets. In

### Going global implications and beneficiaries

Going global implications and beneficiaries 
 
 
(1) The gaps between GDP and GNP may widen 
Rising portions of economics, revenues, and accrued profits derived from overseas 
markets by Chinese companies and their offshore subsidiaries will likely benefit GNP/GNI 
more than GDP over time, akin to the experiences from Japan since the 1980s, from 
which the ratios of GNP over GDP have embarked on a sustained uptrend until now.  
 
(2) FX appreciation pressures are likely to build over time; 
The strong performance of the export sector has significantly bolstered China’s external 
account, and led to massive buildup in trade surplus, which could amount to US$1tn this 
year. This has also in turn put more appreciation pressure on the Rmb, of which the 
starting valuation appears very competitive on a real trade-weighted basis. This 
provides an anchor to our FX Strategist’s call for a gradual strengthening of the Rmb vs 
the USD over time, and eventually reaching the equilibrium real exchange rate at 
5-handle over the next decade.

### (3) Corporate profits could be marginally less fixated on domestic growth

(3) Corporate profits could be marginally less fixated on domestic growth 
Overseas businesses and the resulting revenue streams and cost bases are by nature less 
correlated with the domestic economy but more in sync with the global dynamics, 
thereby incrementally reducing the aggregate market sensitivity to domestic growth 
when foreign revenues become sizable. In an optimistic scenario where the overseas 
margin premium stays robust, that could lead to re-rating potential for export-oriented 
firms vs pure domestic plays.  
 
(4) Rising financing need for overseas investment and expansion via the capital market 
As the non-domestic businesses scale and mature, financing needs in foreign currency 
(operational currency) should rise in tandem. In fact, Dim Sum bond issuance size and 
capital raised through Hong Kong IPOs have risen in recent years, of which the proceeds 
could be mostly used for funding overseas growth and FX hedge purposes. As such, 
equity financing (IPOs and additional follow-ons), spin-offs of offshore subsidiaries by 
their Chinese parent companies/controlling shareholders, and M&A could rise as the 
Going Global trend develops.
