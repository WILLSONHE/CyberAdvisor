1. Two consecutive years of positive returns: a slow(er) bull market is probably in the making 
 
 
China A and H shares have returned 16% and 29% in 2025, respectively, exceeding 
our original forecasts of 13% and 15% published in our 2025 Outlook, and extending the 
rebound from their respective cycle troughs in late 2022 to 30% and 75%.  
The ytd gains have been led by multiple re-rating where index 12m fP/E rose from 
9.9x at the start of 2025 to 12.5x as of now, while fEPS was down by 4% for the year. 
Sectorally, Materials, Health Care, and Comm. Services performed the best while 
Staples, Utilities, and Real Estate lagged. Stylistically, GARP and Growth gained the most, 
contrasting the underperformance by Value and Dividend. These divides underscore the 
abundance of sector and thematic alpha beneath the headline index. 
We expect the bull run to continue, but at a slower pace. We forecast Chinese stocks 
to rise 38% by end-27, led by 14% and 12% profit growth in 2026E and 2027E and 
~10% re-rating potential. This is reminiscent of an equity cycle transition from Hope to 
Growth where both earnings realization and moderate PE expansion in the latter 
typically supersede strong but volatile re-rating gains in the former to drive returns.

### 2. Better trade outcomes have outweighed (fiscal) policy miss

2. Better trade outcomes have outweighed (fiscal) policy miss 
 
 
A challenging external environment for China characterized by higher US tariffs, 
slower GDP growth, a weaker Rmb, but aggressive fiscal stimulus, was GS’s macro base 
case (and a popular investor narrative) that went into our forecasts to start 2025. 
While the US effective tariff rate has indeed risen from 11% to 29% since President 
Trump took office, Chinese exports have grown 5.4% yoy and the Rmb has strengthened 
4% vs. USD ytd. Our economists have also raised their 2025 GDP growth forecast to 
5%, up from 4.5% previously. This may have reduced the urgency for the authorities to 
stimulate domestic demand, with the 2025 augmented fiscal deficit (AFD, Rmb15.5tn, 
11% of GDP) falling short of our economists’ prior forecast (Rmb18tn, 13% of GDP). 
On prevailing US tariff rate and AFD, we estimate that the fair value of MSCI China in a 
2-factor regime should be at around 74, 11% below the current levels, implying that the 
better-than-feared growth backdrop has perhaps outweighed the lukewarm policy 
response in equity pricing, as also reflected by the outperformance of listed exporters 
over consumption equities. The seesaw between tariff and policy will likely remain a 
dominant macro theme going into 2026.

### 3. AI has changed the game for tech equities

3. AI has changed the game for tech equities 
 
 
The launch of DeepSeek-R1 has sparked a powerful rally for Chinese tech companies in 
2025, led by Data & Cloud, Semis, and (AI) Infra & Power, where stock prices have risen 
40% on average and more than US$2tn of market cap has been added. 
The breakthroughs in AI have rewritten the narrative for tech equities. Fundamentally, 
we estimate that widespread AI adoption could drive a 3% annual increase in 
corporate earnings over the next decade via cost savings, enhanced productivity, and 
new revenue opportunities. Valuations have re-rated in the Chinese AI tech ecosystem, 
but they look inexpensive vis-a-vis the US, considering China’s potential upside in 
capex spend and its focus on AI monetization via use-case creation.  
The tech race between the US and China will likely create structural winners along the 
value chain, particularly in parts of the ecosystem where China’s cost advantages are 
high (e.g. power, human capital, LLMs, hardware supply chain) and government support 
is strong due to national security considerations (e.g. semi design and manufacturing).

### 4. China’s Journey to the World: Steady as it goes despite trade headwinds

4. China’s Journey to the World: Steady as it goes despite trade headwinds 
 
 
In our view, China’s export resiliency this year is a strong testament that its exports 
story has evolved—shifting from a low-cost, low-value-added manufacturer for DM  
consumers to one where China is selling more to EM as the final destination, gaining 
global market share in the high-end manufacturing sectors, and now exporting services, 
intellectual property, and culture to the world.  
Overseas revenues of Chinese listed companies have grown from 12% a decade ago to 
16% at present, and could reach 20% by 2030 on our projection, vs. DM/EM average of 
53%/48%. In turn, this could boost earnings of MSCI China by about 1.5% annually 
given higher overseas profitability. 
While protectionist measures from China’s trading partners are valid 
risks/concerns, they are unlikely to derail Chinese companies’ Going Global trend, which 
is underpinned by a highly competitive Rmb (in real effective terms), China’s pivotal role 
in global supply chains and dominance in critical minerals, and improving value 
proposition of Chinese products. 
Our cross-sectional analysis shows that industry leaders with strong innovation 
capabilities are well positioned to compete globally, and could outperform their peers 
in both the export and financial markets. Our Chinese Going Global Leaders portfolio, 
containing 25 GS-Buy-rated companies that on average generate 34% of their revenues 
overseas, has gained 35% ytd, outpacing MSCI China by 9pp.

### 5. Chinese are still spending, but in different pockets of the economy than b…

5. Chinese are still spending, but in different pockets of the economy than before 
 
 
The ailing housing market, falling prices, restrained government spending, and subdued 
household income growth have continued to weigh on domestic consumption, with 
retail sales growing merely 4% yoy ytd and consumer confidence hovering at all-time 
lows. Consumption is widely regarded by investors as a weak link in the economy.  
Consumption is, however, a broad concept, spanning both tradable and non-tradable 
goods, with the latter (services) generally faring better in the post-Covid era on wide 
output gaps (vs. pre-Covid levels) and less pronounced competition, overcapacity, and 
pricing pressures than goods consumption. It also encompasses many niche and 
emerging consumer segments that are not well captured by or apopulated in 
conventional consumption statistics and indicators -notably Entertainment, Specialty 
Retail (e.g. PopMart), Leisure, and Packaged Food (e.g. pet food).  
These “New” consumption industries , as defined by their growth momentum and 
interest from investors, on average grew their net profits by 28% in 1H25, vs. 5% for the 
aggregate consumer cohort (ex-Internet), and have returned 43% ytd, leading MSCI 
China and “Traditional” consumer stocks by 15pp and 39pp respectively.

### 6. All eyes are on inflation: Anti-involution has reignited hopes for reflation

6. All eyes are on inflation: Anti-involution has reignited hopes for reflation 
 
 
The nexus of overcapacity, intense competition, and disinflation has inflicted pain on 
corporate earnings in “involuted” sectors such as Solar, Battery, Chemicals, and 
Cement. “ Anti-involution” was first featured in the July 2024 Politburo meeting and has 
been elevated to the national level since the July 2025 CCFEA meeting. 
Top-down, every 1% rise in PPI could drive 2% of profit growth. Sectorally, we estimate 
that potential supply reduction and industry consolidation could raise profits in 
involuted industries by +50% by 2027 on normalized margins, and boost index earnings 
by 5% by 2028, in-line with sell-side consensus expectations. 
The buy-side community is more skeptical, citing renewed pricing pressures and lack of 
policy follow-through, although “deeply fighting involutionary competition” was 
emphasized in the latest CEWC readout. Overall, the policy upside in Cement, Solar, and 
Chemicals seems attractively priced per our sector framework, and our anti-involution 
beneficiaries have risen 12% since July 1, modestly ahead of MSCI China by 2pp.

### 7. The 15th 5-Year Plan may have re-defined investors’ 5-year plan for Chines…

7. The 15th 5-Year Plan may have re-defined investors’ 5-year plan for Chinese equities  
 
 
China’s 5-Year Plan targets rarely missed. In the past 5 Five-Year Plans (FYP) since 
2001, Chinese policymakers were able to accomplish nearly 90% of their proposed 
numerical growth and development targets. In the 15th FYP proposal published in late 
October, policymakers set technology, security, and people’s livelihood as top priorities 
for China’s development strategy over 2026-30. 
Deciphering and following the FYPs can be rewarding. Our LLM-based textual model 
(FinBERT), which enables us to extract and convert policy signals from the FYP 
documents into long-short strategies, suggests that investors could have generated 13% 
annualized alpha if they had aligned their portfolios with broad policy trends in the past 
quarter century, vs. 6% CAGR by MSCI China.  
Following this logic, we construct our 15th FYP equity universe based on 35 GICS3 
Industries, from which we form our “15th FYP Portfolio”  comprising 50 mid-cap 
stocks from 21 sub-sectors (30 A-shares, 20 offshore). 
These stocks have collectively returned 68% in the past year (compared to 27% by 
MSCI), and are expected by consensus to achieve 30% EPS CAGR over the next two 
years. The portfolio has exhibited a historical 3-year beta of 0.7x (vs MSCI China), and is 
less volatile than well-followed mid/small cap indexes such as ChiNext and CSI1000.

Equity-starving domestic capital is on the move. (1) Southbound flows have

### 9. China’s diversification value is on full display, to both international an…

9. China’s diversification value is on full display, to both international and domestic investors 
 
 
The US is less exceptional than before. Concerns about elevated valuations, high 
market (cap) concentration, AI bubble, and a weakening Dollar trend have motivated 
money managers globally to look for alternatives in non-US markets. China appears 
well-placed for the diversification flows, given its market depth and breadth, and 
conservative investor positioning. China has also exhibited one of the lowest return 
correlations with US equities among key markets and Chinese equities are still priced at 
deep valuation discounts compared with international peers (35%/9% to DM/EM).  
Domestic investors also need to diversify. Individual investors conceptually are far 
away from their optimal efficient frontier, with property/cash accounting for 54%/28% 
of their asset base, vs. 11% in equities. Similarly, institutional investors are 
underexposed to stocks, owning 14% of A-share market cap, vs. 59%/50% in major 
DMs/EMs. With the housing market still finding its bottom, real rates likely trending 
lower, and equities starting to outperform other competing asset classes domestically, 
we believe the structural Chinese capital migration to equities might have begun.

### 10. Many risks still linger , but policy put has flattened the left-tail

10. Many risks still linger , but policy put has flattened the left-tail 
 
 
Besides the well-telegraphed local macro headwinds centering on the property, 
leverage, and demographic nexus, in our recent report, we highlighted 5 risks (investor 
concerns) that could derail the equity uptrend—global recession, AI exuberance,  
US-China tensions, and domestic policy and (dis)inflation risks. 
They, together with the recent macro data softness, have indeed been the main sources 
of volatility and have caused pullbacks this year. But, key China risk factors are either 
well priced or have receded in 2025— the housing sector has shrunk in terms of its 
economic influences, the policy/regulation cycle has turned more pro-growth and 
pro-market, and the reflation prospects have brightened, per GS forecast.  
Overall, it seems clear to us that the macro and equity-market policy put remains in 
effect. It should help reduce left-tail growth and policy risks, thereby compressing 
equity risk premia and shifting the expected fair value of Chinese stocks rightward. We 
would stay invested and accumulate on corrections to better monetize the recovering 
profit cycle and the right-tail optionality.
